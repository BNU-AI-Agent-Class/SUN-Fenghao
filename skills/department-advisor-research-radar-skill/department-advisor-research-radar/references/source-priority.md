# Source Priority

Use this file when deciding which public evidence should drive the final research-direction summary.

## Priority Ladder

### Tier 1: Strongest Current Evidence

Use these first when available:
- recent journal article pages
- DOI-backed publisher pages
- PubMed / Springer / Elsevier / Wiley / ACM / IEEE / Nature / other primary publication pages
- recent conference proceedings or conference program pages
- active grant or project pages hosted by official institutions

Best use:
- determine current research themes
- identify actual methods, populations, and topic clusters
- verify that the person is still active in that area

### Tier 2: Strong Identity And Activity Anchors

- ORCID
- Google Scholar
- official lab pages
- institutional repository pages
- official CV pages

Best use:
- confirm author identity
- find recent publication clusters
- cross-check naming variants

### Tier 3: Institutional News And Public Academic Activity

- school news on papers, grants, awards, lectures, seminars
- department announcements
- center or lab news
- public lecture pages
- conference speaker bios

Best use:
- detect active projects or newly emphasized topics
- update stale faculty profile summaries

### Tier 4: Official Faculty Profile

Use as:
- roster anchor
- identity anchor
- official title and contact source
- baseline statement of long-term areas

Do not assume profile text is current if last update is old.

### Tier 5: Secondary Aggregators

- X-MOL
- ResearchGate
- AMiner
- Baidu Xueshu
- Semantic Scholar profile pages

Use as:
- discovery aids
- cross-reference sources
- identity hints

Do not treat them as sole proof of current direction.

### Tier 6: Media And Interviews

- media interviews
- public-facing feature stories
- outreach pages
- podcast or lecture summaries

Use as:
- context
- translation of academic work into public themes
- supporting evidence for current interests

Do not let them override stronger academic evidence.

## Conflict Handling

When sources disagree:
1. prefer the more recent source
2. prefer the more primary source
3. prefer the source with stronger identity confirmation
4. explain the conflict briefly in the report if it matters

Example:
- official profile says "career psychology"
- 2024-2026 papers center on "privacy decision-making on online platforms"

Then summarize as:
"Publicly visible recent work suggests the faculty member is currently more active in privacy decision-making and applied decision research, while older official materials still emphasize career psychology."

## Sparse-Evidence Rules

If evidence is sparse:
- do not invent subtopics
- keep the direction broad
- say what the inference is based on
- recommend email confirmation when the user's downstream task is admissions or advisor contact

## Time Window

Default recent window: `3-5 years`

Adjust only if:
- the field publishes slowly
- the faculty member is senior and active mainly through books or policy work
- the user explicitly asks for a wider historical frame
